# How Axon's Founder Built a $35bn Business: 10 Lessons from Rick Smith

### 10 Lessons from our hundreds of hours of research on Axon.

*Source: Speedwell Research*

Axon is a leading law enforcement technology solutions provider. Founded by Rick Smith in 1993. Revenues grown from just $3mn in 2000 to almost ~$2bn today. Stock increased ~800x since 2001 or at a 33% CAGR. Today worth over $30bn; founder Rick Smith still owns 5% of the company.

## The 10 Lessons

**1) Know the Best Way to Sell Your Product and You will Only have to Sell it Once.**

Axon initially sold TASERs as one-time capex expenditure — had to fight for "surplus budget" every few years. Discovered the key: move from one-time Capex to regular, on-going "Opex." They sold annual programs packaging TASERs, cartridges, training, service, and free upgrades. Once enshrined in the annual budget as a line item, it is much less likely to be cut. Every year that line item was there; renewal became the path of least resistance.

**2) Solve Ancillary Problems to Create New Businesses and Revenue Streams.**

When the TASER drew hundreds of "ambulance-chaser" lawsuits, Axon engineered a solution: TASER CAM mounted on the TASER, automatically filming the incident when deployed. This gave video evidence that vindicated officers. This directly led to the body cam business — an even better solution. Axon had no idea adding a camera to the TASER would eventually surpass their existing business. Today TASER devices and cartridges are just about 1/3rd of total business.

**3) Build Solutions, not Products.**

Instead of just building a product and focusing on features, Axon focuses on the "job" of each product. Similar to Clay Christensen "Jobs to be Done" framework. When rolling out body cams, they knew software would be key to winning — agencies could have on-officer video without creating more upload/storage/management problems. Competitors solved "how do we give police videos." Axon solved "how can we help police agencies improve accountability and protect themselves."

**4) Stand by your Product and Your Customer.**

When TASER drew lawsuits, Axon aggressively defended all excess use-of-force cases. Built enormous goodwill with law enforcement agencies. This credibility was essential when asking them to trust Axon with sensitive digital evidence in the cloud.

**5) Use One Product to Build Relationships & Trust for Others.**

When Axon released Body Cams, many competitors popped up. Axon's advantage: trust built through defending lawsuits. Critical to convince departments to move sensitive evidence to the cloud. An upstart without history of working with law enforcement would have had a much harder time despite how logical it was on paper.

**6) Make Everyone in the Organization Understand Your Customers.**

A new TASER called the X3 made impressive engineering advancements but in areas customers didn't care about at the cost of things they did care about. Everyone must understand what customers actually value.

**7) Show The Product's Impact Holistically.**

The TASER didn't just incapacitate assailants — it reduced officer injuries and workers' comp costs and increased officers available for field duty. When selling body cams, showed the time cost of uploading competitors' "cheaper" products vs Axon's auto-upload. Draft One AI: frees up ~20% of an officer's time (officers can spend ~40% on reports; Draft One reduces that by ~50%).

**8) Own Distribution.**

By 2016 rationalized distributors from 23 (2011) to just 3. Taking control of distribution allowed a more dedicated salesforce that could better cross-sell. Warranty sales went up, gross margins improved, direct channel to customers for feedback.

**9) If Something Doesn't Work Initially, Understand Why and Do Not be Afraid to Try Again.**

The original TASER was invented in 1975 but never reached wide use. A second attempt in the mid-80s (Tasertron) failed — device worked only ~56% of the time. The TASER treaded mediocrity for 2+ decades until Rick Smith saw its potential. Just because a business didn't work doesn't mean the product was a bad idea.

**10) When you have a Strong Intuition, Stick with it Through the Doubts.**

Axon's largest business today (body cam + Evidence.com software) was almost shut down just before it started seeing success. Around 2013, most employees thought they should exit the body cam business. Rick Smith disagreed and charged ahead. The lesson: conviction matters when you are closest to the customer problem.
