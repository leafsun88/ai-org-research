# AI-powered police body cameras training to detect over 7,000 people on 'high risk' watch list

*Source: Associated Press*

Police body cameras equipped with artificial intelligence have been programmed to recognize the faces of approximately **7,000 individuals on a 'high risk' watch list** in Edmonton, Canada. This represents a live trial to assess whether facial recognition technology, often criticized for being overly invasive, can be integrated into law enforcement practices across North America.

## Background

Despite Axon Enterprise voicing serious ethical reservations about facial recognition six years ago, the pilot initiative — activated December 2025 — has sparked concerns well beyond Edmonton (population over one million; northernmost major city on the continent).

**Barry Friedman**, a former chair of Axon's AI ethics board who previously advocated for a halt to facial recognition in 2019, expressed apprehension to the AP. He believes Axon is proceeding without sufficient public discussion, testing, and expert evaluation regarding societal dangers and privacy issues.

## The Pilot Program

- **Edmonton Police Service** activated it to enhance patrol officer safety
- Watch list: **6,341 names** flagged as "violent or assaultive," "armed and dangerous," or "high-risk offender" status, plus **724 additional individuals with at least one significant criminal warrant**
- Duration: Until end of December (daylight hours only due to winter/lighting challenges)
- ~50 officers involved will **not be informed of matches in real-time** — data analyzed later at the station
- Future scenarios: could alert officers to dangerous individuals during active investigations

## Key Concerns

**From Friedman (former Axon ethics board chair):**
> "This is a decision that should not rest solely with police agencies or vendors. A pilot is a commendable idea, but it should come with transparency and accountability, which appears to be lacking in this instance."

**On accuracy:** Axon acknowledged inherent limitations of facial recognition — distance, lighting, angle, and less accuracy for individuals with darker skin tones. Every match will undergo human review; part of testing is determining oversight requirements for human reviewers to mitigate known risks.

**Third-party vendor:** Axon does not develop its own facial recognition AI and has not revealed the third-party vendor it collaborates with.

**Privacy oversight:** Alberta's Information and Privacy Commissioner Diane McLeod received a privacy impact assessment from Edmonton police on December 2, 2025 — the same day as the announcement. Reviewing it as "high sensitivity" personal data project.

## Broader Context

- Axon's 2019 commitment: not to put face recognition in body cameras until technology proved more accurate and legal/privacy issues were resolved — described at the time as the **first public safety tech company to make this commitment**
- CEO Rick Smith asserts the company has engaged in "controlled, lab-based research" since then, which has significantly improved accuracy
- Since 2023, Axon and other tech companies resumed facial recognition development after a pause driven by 2020-era backlash

**Motorola Solutions** stated it could also incorporate facial recognition into police body cameras but has "consciously chosen not to deploy this feature for proactive identification based on its ethical guidelines."

**If the Edmonton pilot expands**, it could significantly influence policing practices globally — Axon is the leading supplier of body cameras in the U.S. and markets increasingly to Canada and beyond.

## Historical Notes

- Axon's AI ethics board resigned in 2022 in protest of the Taser-equipped school drone concept
- Multiple U.S. states and cities have moved to limit facial recognition by law enforcement
- EU: prohibited real-time public face-scanning by police (except serious crimes like kidnapping/terrorism)
- UK: began testing in London a decade ago; 1,300 arrests using it in past two years
