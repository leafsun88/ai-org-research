# Axon CEO Rick Smith: Can AI and Training Help Replace Guns with Non-Lethal Weapons?

*Source: LinkedIn / Fortt Knox (Jon Fortt), AI-assisted summary*

Rick Smith says Axon's hardest problem today is the same one he started the company to solve: making the bullet obsolete. Axon has publicly committed to cutting gun deaths in policing in half by 2032, a mission requiring both technological and cultural breakthroughs.

## TODAY'S TOUGHEST PROBLEM

For Smith, the toughest problem is unchanged from Axon's founding mission: create a non-lethal weapon reliable enough that officers choose it first, reducing police shootings nationwide. The technical side remains daunting — current Tasers don't always pierce heavy clothing. But Smith says the new **Apollo cartridge** is close to solving that. Culturally, officers must believe the device will work when their lives are on the line.

> "We basically have to create a non-lethal weapon that performs as reliably as a nine millimeter bullet, so that police don't have to go to their guns much anymore. As you can imagine, that is both tough technically and culturally when you think about the relationship Americans have with their guns and the belief that it is the ultimate device to save your life in a pinch."

## ORIGIN STORY

Rick Smith was born in Santa Monica, raised mostly in Denver, studied **neurobiology at Harvard**, and envisioned building robotic limbs. After two students from his high school were shot and killed in a road-rage incident, he became obsessed with non-lethal alternatives. While in grad school, he discovered the NASA scientist behind the Taser and tracked him down via 411.

> "I started digging around, and I discovered this NASA scientist from the '60s had been developing this device called the Taser. And one thing led to another. And next thing, I'm knocking on his front door. I'm 23 years old. He's 73."

## DEATH VALLEY

Axon's near-death moment came in 2005: an SEC investigation triggered by a coordinated short-seller campaign. Overnight, sales dropped 50%. Litigation skyrocketed from zero to 400 cases. Smith drew on a lesson from his father: surface the truth fast.

> "I went to our lawyers and I said, 'Look, I want to help the SEC investigators get their job done. I want to go meet with them.' And my lawyer said, 'You are crazy. Nobody goes to voluntarily testify in front of a government agency.' I said, 'I don't have time for that. We won't survive. We've got to get this done.'"

The episode reshaped Axon's culture and propelled the pivot into body cameras and cloud software.

## CORE BELIEF

Smith's core belief centers on candor, ethical clarity, and long-term thinking — values forged in Axon's toughest moments. Company values today: **Win Right, Aim Far, Expect Candor**.

> "'Win right' is one. That's what we tell our employees. Hey, we want to win, but nobody's going to jail over this. We've been through the terrible headlines. When we win, we're going to do it a way we're proud of."

## STRATEGIC IMPERATIVE: 2026 IS ALL ABOUT AI

Smith is unequivocal: 2026 is all about AI. Axon isn't building foundational models, but instead layers models from OpenAI and others onto real-world audio, video, and sensor data. This enables auto-generated police reports, real-time translation, instant policy lookup, and AI "copilot" assistance.

> "Body cameras started as a device to protect your butt, basically. To collect evidence that you would use later in court. Then we turned it into a connected device where now it's live streaming video. Now we're layering AI on top of that where these are audio video sensors, your AI buddy, that can help you do all the data management tasks."

Officers spend roughly **half their day writing reports**; eliminating even a portion is equivalent to increasing the size of the force. Axon's **"AI era plan,"** which bundles future AI services into long-term contracts, has become its fastest-growing product.

## INTERNATIONAL INFLECTION

VR-based training, extended Taser range, and multi-shot capability are shaping early international success. Countries like Norway and the UK are considering Tasers as primary weapons instead of adding guns — signaling a potential inflection point globally.
