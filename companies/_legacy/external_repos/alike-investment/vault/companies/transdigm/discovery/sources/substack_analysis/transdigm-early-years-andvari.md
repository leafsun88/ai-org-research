# TransDigm: The Early Years

"When you think of TransDigm, you want to think of a private equity-like model being run in the public market." -- Nick Howley, TransDigm Founder and CEO, February 8, 2013

## Main Thesis

A management team that consistently applies an unchanging, proven value-creation strategy transforms good businesses into exceptional ones. This alchemical process produces results that seem almost unbelievable when examined closely.

TransDigm represents a remarkable case study in business consistency. Over four decades, the company's fundamental strategy has remained virtually unchanged, distinguishing it in an environment where corporate identities typically transform every 10-15 years.

## Historical Background: Imo Industries

TransDigm originated from a 1993 management-led leveraged buyout of Imo Industries' aerospace division. The acquisition included four established businesses: Wiggins Connectors (1925), Adel Fasteners (1938), Aeroproducts (1935), and Controlex (1945).

Imo's lineage traces to 1890 Stockholm, where Gustaf de Laval founded a steam turbine company. The American operation, incorporated in 1901, became a major supplier of power-generating equipment and naval gears. Transamerica acquired the company in 1962, integrated it with other manufacturers, and eventually spun it off as Imo Industries in 1987.

During the late 1980s, newly independent Imo pursued aggressive acquisition strategies, growing revenues from $330 million (1987) to roughly $1 billion (1991). However, this debt-fueled expansion combined with asbestos litigation — approximately 7,000 lawsuits — forced asset sales. The aerospace division represented one such divestment.

## The Acquisition and Early Performance

Co-founders Nick Howley and Doug Peacock, working with private equity firm Kelso & Company, completed the $56 million acquisition in September 1993. The deal involved $36 million in debt and $25 million in equity (with $5 million in banking fees), establishing an initial leverage ratio of 3.47x net debt-to-EBITDA.

Early financials showed declining revenues from 1988-1996, reflecting post-Cold War defense cutbacks, reduced commercial aircraft production, and inventory corrections. Despite revenue challenges, the division maintained impressive EBITDA margins in the high 20s throughout the early 1990s.

## The Odyssey Years and Strategic Clarity

Five years after Kelso's investment, Odyssey Partners acquired TransDigm. Under Odyssey's ownership, the company's tri-partite value creation playbook crystallized: value-based pricing, profitable new business generation, and continuous productivity improvements.

Howley recalled that acquisitions accelerated during this period. The first acquisition under Odyssey — Adams Rite Aerospace in April 1999 — exemplified TransDigm's emerging thesis. The repeatable formula involved acquiring proprietary aerospace businesses with significant aftermarket content, where management could see clear paths to private equity-like returns.

## Strategic Consistency Over Decades

Since at least 2003, TransDigm has described its business focus identically: proprietary aerospace components with significant aftermarket content. Organizational philosophy slides from 2003, 2014, and 2024 reveal striking continuity in company culture and operational methodology.

This consistency extended to public market performance. After the 2006 IPO, TransDigm maintained approximately 30% annualized returns (2006-2021) — private equity-like performance with public company liquidity.

## Summary

Kevin Stein, TransDigm's third CEO, summarized the enduring strategy: "We follow a consistent long-term strategy, specifically. First, we own and operate proprietary aerospace businesses with significant aftermarket content. Second, we utilize a simple, well-proven, value-based operating methodology. Third, we have a decentralized organizational structure and unique compensation system closely aligned with shareholders. Fourth, we acquire businesses that fit the strategy where we see a clear path to PE-like returns."

Playing in the right industry while consistently executing the right strategy over extended periods generates exceptional shareholder returns.
