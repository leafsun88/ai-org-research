# Negative Equity Companies: TransDigm Case Study

Fernandez explores how companies with negative shareholder equity are not necessarily in financial distress. He explains that negative equity often results from aggressive capital returns to shareholders through dividends and share buybacks — a strategy viable only for high-quality businesses.

## The Balance Sheet Fundamentals

The basic accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) shows that equity comprises retained earnings (NI minus dividends paid), treasury stock, and other components. Negative equity emerges when companies return more cash to shareholders than they retain.

## TransDigm Case Study

TransDigm Group (TDG), an aircraft components manufacturer, exemplifies this phenomenon. Despite negative equity, the company achieved a CAGR of 20% over ten years — meaning $1,000 invested in 2015 would grow to approximately $6,000 by 2025.

The company maintains exceptional financial metrics:
- Strong cash generation capability
- Return on Capital Employed (ROCE) reflecting superior competitive advantages
- Consistent shareholder value delivery

## Valuation Multiple Impact

Buybacks artificially inflate price-to-book ratios. A company returning capital improves its multiple while deteriorating book value, which can paradoxically indicate business strength rather than weakness.

## Other Quality Companies with Negative Equity

- McDonald's Corporation (MCD)
- Domino's Pizza (DPZ)
- Lowe's Companies (LOW)
- Dell Technologies (DELL)

Each demonstrates strong returns on capital and solid long-term shareholder performance despite negative book values.
