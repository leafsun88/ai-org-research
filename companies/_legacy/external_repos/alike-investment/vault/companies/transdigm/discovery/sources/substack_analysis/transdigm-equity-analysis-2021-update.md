# TransDigm Equity Analysis (11-30-2021 Update)

## Introduction

TransDigm Inc manufactures aircraft components for both commercial and military applications. The company operates through two revenue streams: original equipment manufacturing (OEM) and aftermarket sales. Aircraft typically last 25-30 years while TransDigm products have 50-year lifespans, enabling sustained aftermarket revenue. The aftermarket segment generates superior margins and represents the bulk of EBITDA despite comprising only 49% of revenue.

TransDigm produces three main product categories: Power & Control (electronic and mechanical systems), Airframe (structural components), and Non-aviation (related items like headsets and fuel valves). Products are typically small components rarely exceeding $100,000, positioning them as overlooked line items in multi-million-dollar aircraft purchases.

## Pricing Power

TransDigm's primary competitive advantage stems from pricing power. Approximately 80% of revenue comes from sole-source products where they have complete control over the price of their products and have the autonomy to increase prices and margins over time.

Aircraft manufacturers and airlines prioritize reliability over cost for small components. Since Boeing 737s cost ~$114.7 million and Airbus A320s cost ~$101 million, negotiating $700 parts represents negligible savings.

## M&A Strategy

Nicholas Howley, Executive Chairman, operates TransDigm as a private equity fund. The company acquires businesses at approximately 12x EV/EBITDA, deploys management expertise to streamline operations, then divests underperforming units.

The 2019 Esterline acquisition exemplified this model. Purchased for $3.9 billion at 12.3x EV/EBITDA, TransDigm sold off Souriau-Sunbank ($920 million) and Esterline Interface Technology ($190 million), ultimately reducing the remaining business to 6.5x EV/EBITDA. TransDigm maintains 7.6x net debt-to-EBITDA, financed through LIBOR loans at 2.25%, prioritizing acquisitions over debt reduction. Since 2005, the company acquired 49 businesses.

## Competitive Moat

- **Economies of scale:** Hundreds of manufacturing facilities with specialized equipment and 14,000+ employees create substantial barriers
- **Industry stickiness:** Long-term contracts (exceeding 30 years) and switching costs embed relationships
- **Patents and IP:** Patented components often represent the sole compatible option for aircraft
- **Management quality:** Leadership team including CEO Kevin Stein, CFO Michael Lisman, and M&A head Bernt Iversen

## Aftermarket Economics

Aftermarket sales generate gross margins exceeding 70%, accounting for 90% of EBITDA while representing just 49% of revenue.

## Valuation

As of November 26, 2021, TransDigm traded at 18.26x EV/EBITDA and 8.64x EV/Revenue. Using a WACC of 9.1%, the DCF analysis implies a $740 intrinsic value, representing a 32% margin of safety.

## Management Incentives

Compensation consists primarily of stock options tied to 10% Annual Operating Performance growth targets. Cash compensation is approximately 30% below industry peers, aligning management interests with shareholder value creation.

## Risk Factors

Government regulation of M&A and monopoly designations present material downside risk. Unlike technology companies where breakups create value, TransDigm's operational leverage model depends on consolidated ownership.
