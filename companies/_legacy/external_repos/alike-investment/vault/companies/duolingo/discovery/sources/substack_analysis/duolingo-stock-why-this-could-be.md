Introduction:

From an investor’s perspective, the main concern in this report is the slowdown in MAU user growth, something management has acknowledged openly.

Duolingo relies heavily on expanding its active user base, because the long term economics come from converting a small percentage of those users into paying subscribers.

During 2025, daily active user growth began to slow, and management now expects around 20 percent DAU growth in 2026. That is still strong for a platform of this size, but it is slower than previous years and suggests the growth engine may be maturing. Management also admitted that some recent monetization changes, such as ads and feature restrictions, introduced friction in the product and may have reduced engagement and user growth.

As a result, the company plans to slow monetization efforts temporarily and invest more heavily in user growth, product improvements and AI features to restore growth. In the near term, this will likely put pressure on margins and revenue growth.

Another risk is the uncertainty surrounding the company’s strategy. Management is currently experimenting with pricing, subscription tiers, AI capabilities, and the balance between free and paid experiences. This means the business is entering a period of significant product experimentation.

While this approach has historically worked well for Duolingo, it also creates execution risk. If user growth does not meaningfully reaccelerate, the company could end up with slower revenue growth and lower margins after increasing investment. There is also a longer term competitive risk, as AI powered tutoring tools and language models could lower barriers to entry in digital education.

The bull case, however, remains powerful.

Duolingo is already the dominant global (language) learning platform, with more than 50 million daily active users and roughly 85 percent share of usage among language learning apps. That scale creates strong brand recognition and network effects.

Despite that reach, penetration is still surprisingly low. In the United States, only about 2 percent of internet users use Duolingo on a given day, suggesting significant room for expansion globally. If the company successfully reaches its goal of 100 million daily active users by 2028, the economic potential could be enormous.

There is also substantial upside in monetization. Today, only about 10 percent of monthly active users pay for a subscription, leaving a long runway for conversion improvements. New AI driven features, such as conversational tutoring and personalized instruction, could make the product far more effective and increase willingness to pay.

At the same time, Duolingo is expanding beyond languages into areas like math, chess, and music, gradually transforming the platform into a broader digital education ecosystem.

My bull case is simple.

If Duolingo continues to grow its user base, improves monetization gradually, and expands into new subjects, the company has a real chance to dominate the global online learning market.

The ultra-bull case is laid out here by Marc Andreessen:

The main issue I see today is that Duolingo is still primarily a supplement to learning, rather than a replacement for traditional education. Even if the platform becomes more capable over time, schools serve another role that apps cannot easily replace: they function as childcare. Because of that, I do not expect Duolingo to replace schools anytime soon.

However, that does not limit the opportunity. Even as a supplement, the business can still become extremely large, especially if Duolingo continues to dominate the category as it does today.

A bigger debate is the size of the market itself. How many people actually want to learn? My view is that the market is likely much larger than investors assume. Management consistently says they see no signs of saturation, and they believe the business can grow many multiples larger than it is today. This is reinforced by the co-founder’s recent comment that Duolingo is only about 1% into its long term vision, suggesting the company still sees enormous room to expand across the broader education space.

In my view, investors tend to underestimate this opportunity because they assume the market for learning is small. That assumption may simply be wrong.

One area investors worry about is monetization. Personally, I do not see this as a major problem. Management has highlighted several clear levers to increase revenue:

Advertising: Duolingo has barely focused on ads so far, leaving significant room to increase ARPU.

Subscriptions: The company still lacks basic options such as a monthly subscription tier.

Conversion rates: Only about 10% of users pay, compared with roughly 50% for consumer subscription platforms like Spotify.

Because of this gap, management believes there is substantial room to improve conversion over time. If they succeed, monetization could increase significantly even without massive user growth.

Key Investor Concern - Slowing MAU Growth:

The key headline for Duolingo in Q425 was monthly active users declining. Management clearly acknowledged a deceleration in daily active user growth through 2025, and that trend will carry into early 2026. That is the core issue.

Here is what management has actually said about it, distilled from the call.

They observed that MAU growth slowed throughout 2025. They now expect roughly 20 percent year over year MAU growth in 2026, which is slower than prior years. This is not framed as a crisis, but as a clear deceleration compared to their historic trajectory.

Why?

Management pointed to three main drivers.

First, monetization friction.

Luis von Ahn admitted something refreshingly candid: they increased monetization by adding friction to the free experience. More ads. More paywalls. More nudges to upgrade. That works in the short term. It boosts bookings. But friction also slows user growth. They explicitly said they may have pushed that lever too far relative to DAU expansion.

In his own framing, the business is both “under monetized” and “over monetized” at the same time. Under monetized because only about 10 percent of MAUs pay. Over monetized because the way they increased revenue was by making the free product less smooth. That tension likely contributed to slower active user expansion.

Second, law of large numbers.

They are simply much bigger now. They have over 50 million daily active users. When you reach that scale, percentage growth rates naturally compress unless you unlock new channels or markets. This is a statistical reality, not necessarily a structural weakness.

Third, normal engagement churn dynamics.

When asked whether AI translation tools or chatbots caused the slowdown, management rejected that explanation. They said churn survey responses have not changed materially. The most common reason people leave is still “I got busy,” which in translation often means shifting attention to other apps and media.

Importantly, they also explicitly dismissed concerns about:

Market saturation:

Penetration in the US is about 2 percent of internet users on a daily basis. In the UK 3 percent. Germany 4 percent. They argue that even if all countries merely matched US penetration, DAUs would more than double. So management does not believe saturation is the cause.

AI translation competition:

Luis argued that translation quality between major languages has been “essentially perfect” for many years, yet Duolingo still grew massively during that period. He frames (language) learning as either a hobby or a necessity, not something replaced by Google Translate or AI tools.

So to answer directly:

Monthly active users did not fall off a cliff, but growth slowed. Management attributes this primarily to deliberate monetization decisions that increased friction in the free experience, combined with natural scaling effects. They do not blame AI, new competitors, or saturation.

Now here is the interesting strategic pivot.

Instead of short term monetization, they are choosing to re-accelerate user growth. They are:

Improving the free experience

Reducing friction

Moving premium AI features like Video Call down from Max into Super

Investing heavily in math, chess, and new subjects

Accepting slower bookings growth and lower margins in 2026

This is a classic platform trade off: short term monetization versus long term network size.

Management is betting that in an AI world, scale plus distribution wins. If AI tutoring becomes extremely powerful, the company with the largest engaged user base will compound advantage fastest. So they want the “bigger pie” first, monetization second.

Whether that works depends on two things:

Can they truly re-accelerate engagement by reducing friction?

Can they later monetize at higher rates without repeating the same growth drag?

That is the tension to watch.

If you step back, this is less about “users declining” and more about a strategic rebalancing between growth and monetization in an AI transition era. The interesting question is not whether MAUs dipped for a quarter, but whether AI driven improvements can meaningfully increase retention and word of mouth over the next two years. That is where the real experiment is happening.

However, the underlying conversion dynamics look materially stronger. Snapchat converts roughly 5 percent of its daily active users into paying customers, whereas Duolingo converts about 9 percent of monthly active users. More importantly, around 22 to 23 percent of Duolingo’s daily active users are paying subscribers.

That difference is meaningful. In absolute terms, it translated to approximately 11.5 million paid subscribers for Duolingo in the fourth quarter of 2025. These figures suggest that, despite the relatively low penetration rate across the total user base, Duolingo’s most engaged users are converting at a much higher rate than platforms like Snapchat.

At a surface level, this indicates that Duolingo may not face the same structural monetisation problem. In fact, it provides some optimism that the company could improve conversion further over time, particularly as the quality and effectiveness of the product improves. What they usually do is they try performance marketing to try to grow fast, but because our free product is so good, they usually cannot make their performance marketing work if they are to have a free product. What then they do, is they put a paywall on it. Then that just really caps them.

Luis von Ahn, Co-founder and Chief Executive Officer, Duolingo: It’s just been really hard for entrants. 

I mean, look, the reality is that over the span of Duolingo, we have probably seen, I don’t know, 400 apps come up and try to compete with us, and I’m making that number up. It’s hundreds. And we just don’t see them grow again, because we have a very large distribution.

Therefore, whatever the bears argue, Duolingo does not appear easy to replicate. If it were, competitors would likely have done so already. The CEO expands on this point:

It just turns out that people really, really like doing things like playing Candy Crush or doing Duolingo, in part because of the animations, et cetera. If, if you’re just asking, are we gonna be a thing in a chatbot? I just don’t think you can get the engagement that we can get inside, like a text-based chatbot. 

You know, again, I just don’t know what the future is gonna look like, but this is. You know what? At the moment, we feel pretty confident that the right investments are to continue making a beautiful experience that is very visual, because that’s just what keeps people engaged. Yeah, in terms of AI native, I mean, we’ve always. Ultimately, we’re a technology company. We’ve been really trying to adopt AI and technology as fast as possible.

Conclusion:

In short, Duolingo delivered an exceptional quarter and a very strong year. The market reaction, however, reflects concern about the company’s short term shift away from aggressive monetisation.

Management is intentionally prioritising two things instead: massively expanding the user base and improving learning effectiveness. As an investor, this is exactly what I want to see. The company is obsessively focused on the end user and on delivering more value per dollar, with the goal of building the best learning platform in the world.

It would be far more concerning if the company focused on short term Wall Street expectations and missed the larger structural opportunity created by AI. Instead, management is clearly playing the long game. They are willing to accept near term pressure in order to strengthen the platform, expand the moat, and build a much larger business over time.

The market sell off largely reflects this strategic choice. Management is deliberately sacrificing short term monetisation to prioritise long term growth. The strategy is simple: scale first, monetise later. By expanding the ecosystem today, the company aims to become far more dominant in global education over time.

Management also highlighted several encouraging points. First, they are not seeing any signs of market saturation. In other words, they do not see a ceiling on growth any time soon. Second, they do not view other education companies as the primary competition. The real competition is the attention economy, things like social media, games, and entertainment.

That challenge is real, but the potential market is enormous. Global education involves billions of people. The key question is how many of them will eventually use Duolingo. It is not unreasonable to think that number could ultimately reach the billions.

Today the platform still sees itself as a supplement to traditional education and is targeting 100 million daily active users by 2028. Yet investors may be underestimating how large this shift could become. Even as a supplement, the platform already serves an enormous global audience.

Over time, Duolingo’s relentless focus on improving how people learn, and doing it better than anyone else, could become a durable competitive advantage that is extremely difficult to replicate.

Overall, I was very pleased with the report. The strategy management is pursuing appears to be the strongest long term path for the business.
