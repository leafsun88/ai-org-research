Company

DUOL is the leading mobile learning platform globally, best known for its short, fun, and engaging courses.

Its mission is to develop the best education in the world and make it universally available. The company plans to achieve this by offering a mobile-first, AI-powered, and gamified learning experience. 

By making quality education available for all, DUOL can help users — both the rich and poor — unlock vast economic opportunities, build deeper human connections, and open the door to new experiences.

Learners love DUOL due to three key elements:

Low barriers to start learning — all courses are bite-sized and free, promoting initial action.

Gamification features — fun characters, daily streaks, and Experience Points help learners stay motivated, maximizing engagement.

Personalization powered by AI — lessons adapt according to the learners’ strengths and weaknesses, optimizing learning progress.

DUOL is widely recognized for its language courses, which span over 40 languages focused on reading, writing, listening, and speaking. Personally, I used DUOL to learn a little bit of Japanese (Japan is one of my favorite countries to visit).

The company also developed the Duolingo English Test, an online English proficiency test that users can take for reasons such as university admissions, work visas, and job applications. Over 6,000 institutions worldwide, including 24 of the top 25 undergraduate programs in the US, accept the Duolingo English Test as proof of English proficiency.

In addition to languages, DUOL also offers math (launched 2022), music (launched 2023), and chess (launched 2025) courses within the same app.

DUOL primarily operates a freemium business model — users can sign up for the subscription tier that suits their budgets and learning needs:

Free: All of DUOL’s courses are free to access, but the free tier includes ads, limited Hearts (mistakes cost Hearts; run out and you must wait or refill using Gems), and some restricted practice/review features.

Super Duolingo: This is the standard paid tier, which removes all ads, gives unlimited Hearts, and offers other benefits. This tier costs about $13/month or $84/year in the US. There’s also a Super Family Plan, which supports up to 6 accounts for $120/year. 

Duolingo Max: The premium tier that includes all Super benefits plus exclusive AI-powered features like Roleplay (text- and scenario-based conversations) and Video Call with Lily (unstructured conversational speaking practice). This tier costs about $30/month or $168/year. The Max Family Plan is priced at about $240/year.

Based on my initial research, I think DUOL has two distinct moats:

Intangible Asset (Wide): The Duolingo brand is synonymous with language learning. Its mobile app is the top-grossing app in the Education category on both Google Play and the Apple App Store. In that sense, I believe DUOL has a strong brand and technology advantage, as observed by their increasing user and engagement rates. More on those later.

Network Effects (Narrow): DUOL has a massive user base of 135M+ Monthly Active Users (MAUs) and 50M+ Daily Active Users (DAUs), generating huge amounts of learning data. This fuels better AI, personalization, and lessons — creating a flywheel where more users make the app smarter and more engaging, attracting even more users. While not a classic direct network effect (like social media platforms or e-commerce marketplaces), DUOL’s data scale, strong brand, and habit-forming features create a narrow network effect moat that’s difficult for new competitors to overcome quickly.

In a nutshell, DUOL offers free, gamified, and personalized lessons at the tip of your fingertips, disrupting the traditional education system, which is notorious for being too expensive, structured, and standardized.

Fundamentals

Growth

DUOL’s growth has been nothing short of spectacular.

In the latest quarter, Revenue was $272M, up 41% YoY. As you can see, growth has consistently been in the 40% range over the last few years, which is no easy feat for any company.

Keep in mind that DUOL’s Revenue base is primarily made up of stable, predictable Subscription Revenue (about 85% of total Revenue as of Q3). The company also generates Revenue from other sources, such as the sale of the Duolingo English Test, in-app purchases (such as Streak Freezes and Timer Boosts), and its advertising inventory.

Since Subscription Revenue is recognized ratably over the lifetime of the subscription, Revenue may not be the best metric to assess DUOL’s actual growth. Instead, we look at Bookings, which represents money paid upfront from its subscription offering and other sources of income. 

In Q3, Bookings were $282M, up 33% YoY, reflecting strong brand momentum and DUOL’s effectiveness in converting free users to paying users.

Despite record Bookings, we can see that growth is decelerating meaningfully. Given that Bookings is an indicator of future Revenue growth, the slowdown in Bookings meant slower Revenue growth moving forward. This is a major reason why the stock has done so poorly recently.

To make matters worse, DUOL’s user metrics are slowing down as well:

MAUs were 135.3M, up 20% YoY.

DAUs were 50.5M, up 36% YoY.

Paid Subscribers were 11.5M, up 34% YoY.

Let’s focus on DAUs, as it’s arguably the most important indicator of platform engagement and retention, as well as future Bookings and Revenue growth.
