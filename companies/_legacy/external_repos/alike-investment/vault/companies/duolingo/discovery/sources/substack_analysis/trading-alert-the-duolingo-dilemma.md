Few things confuse more investors like a company delivering record-breaking results, only to get punished for it. That’s exactly what happened with Duolingo (NASDAQ: DUOL) this quarter. 

The language-learning giant posted a blowout Q3 2025: daily active users surged past 50 million, revenue soared 41% year-over-year, and profitability expanded, all while guiding to full-year margins near its long-term target range. Yet instead of rewarding that execution, the market responded with a brutal -20% sell-off.

Since our last coverage, Duolingo has consistently outperformed expectations with durable growth, disciplined execution, and one of the most underappreciated moats in consumer tech. Heading into this print, we reiterated our bullish stance, anchored in its flywheel and robust free cash flow engine.

Now, let’s be clear, results are still playing out, and we may yet be proven wrong. But based on the numbers delivered so far, we believe we’re firmly on track for attractive long-term returns. However, this is what happens when betting on a high growth story, this is what the Market believes, any feeling of deceleration and the stock gets rerated beautifully. Which brings us to the real question: is this just another tantrum from Mr. Market, or a rare entry point into a beautifully managed compounder?

In this earnings digest, we’ll unpack what the market missed, what we’re watching closely, and what we plan to do next, starting with an updated Valuation model and full Financial Excel, available below for paid subscribers.

Q3 Results at a Glance 

Milestone User Growth: Daily active users (DAUs) surpassed 50 million for the first time. DAUs grew +36% year-over-year in Q3, on top of an exceptional Q3 last year, reaching record engagement levels.

Soaring Revenue: Quarterly revenue jumped +41% YoY, continuing Duolingo’s streak of ~40%+ growth. Both DAUs and revenue are up over 40% through the first nine months of 2025, showcasing robust top-line momentum.

Expanding Profitability: Adjusted EBITDA margins remained strong (~29% guidance for FY2025) and profitability expanded alongside growth. The company even reported a GAAP profit beat this quarter (helped by a one-time item), highlighting improving operating leverage.

Q4 Outlook: Management guided for Q4 revenue of ~$273-277 and full-year 2025 revenue just over $1.03B, implying ~30% and 38% YoY growth for the quarter and for the year, respectively. They anticipate adjusted EBITDA margins of ~28% for 2025, near their long-term target, all while investing in future growth.

By all accounts, these results were excellent, yet the market reacted by slashing the stock price ~20% in a day.

Why the Stock Plunged 20%: Market Misreads AI Fears 

Despite what looked like a blowout quarter, Duolingo’s share price tumbled dramatically (about -20%) immediately after earnings. We believe this selloff was driven by short-term panic and a misunderstanding of Duolingo’s trajectory, rather than any fundamental deterioration. Here’s what likely spooked investors and why we think those fears are overdone:

Lower Growth Guidance: 

Management guided to $273-277 million in revenue and $329.5-335.5 in bookings, which implies ~30% YoY growth, a visible step down from the +40% growth clocked in the previous two quarters. 

As the chart below shows, quarterly revenue has continued to rise consistently, from $178 million in Q2 to $275 million projected in Q4, but the growth rate line is bending downward, and that’s what spooked the market. For a company that’s delivered 40-50% top-line growth like clockwork, this shift, even if it’s off a larger base, is perceived as a gear change. Investors saw a similar knee-jerk reaction with Netflix years ago: a high-growth name slows just slightly (0.5% loss on subscribers), and the stock gets punished as if the story is broken. But in Duolingo’s case, the fundamentals, ~38% full-year growth and ~29% adjusted EBITDA margin, remain stellar. 

Here’s what matters: this moderation is intentional. Management is prioritizing learning outcomes and product quality to build a deeper, stickier user base. They are not pulling back from growth, they’re investing into durability, without sacrificing profitability. CFO Matt Skaruppa explicitly said that retention remains strong, monetization is healthy, and this rebalancing will not materially affect margin targets. This is long-term product stewardship, not a slowdown in demand. Finally, CEO and co-founder Luins von Ahn expressed it wonderfully:

“We have made a slight shift over the last quarter in how we invest, and we’re investing a lot more in long-term things because we see that as such a big opportunity ahead of us”.

Growth “Slowdown” Jitters: 

After many quarters of >40% DAU growth, the 36% YoY DAU increase in Q3 had some observers worried about deceleration. The market is confusing a natural growth fluctuation with a trend. In reality, Duolingo’s user growth has never been a straight line, it comes in waves. Recall that in mid-2021, DAU growth dipped to ~0%, only to surge back after Duolingo tweaked its product to drive engagement. Management consciously balances growth, monetization, and teaching quality; some quarters they prioritize other levers. 

A 36% increase on a larger base is still phenomenal, and management signaled no erosion in user retention or engagement. In fact, CFO Matt Skaruppa stated on the call that overall platform retention remains strong, with “no real changes to that”, directly refuting the idea that users are churning away. The slight slowdown in growth is more a reflection of Duolingo’s cycle of optimizations (not a permanent stall), something this company has orchestrated before to come out even stronger.

AI Competition Narrative: 

The hottest fear is that generative AI (like ChatGPT) will disrupt Duolingo’s language learning platform. This AI hysteria hit other ed-tech names too. Some investors assumed Duolingo’s slower DAU growth meant ChatGPT or other AI tutors are siphoning users. We see zero evidence of this. Management was emphatic that ChatGPT is not impacting user retention or engagement. Unlike Q&A-based services that ChatGPT can replace, Duolingo offers a gamified, curriculum-based learning experience built on proprietary data and pedagogy. The CEO Luis von Ahn reminded investors that Duolingo is improving learning outcomes every year, and now with AI they can do so even faster. In his words: 

“Whenever we fixate on a metric, we are very good at moving it”.

This team has massive data advantages (over 1 billion exercises completed daily by users) and an app that people love coming back to. If anything, Duolingo is using AI as a tailwind, integrating AI tutors and features, rather than being a victim of it. The market’s knee-jerk lumping of Duolingo with “AI-vulnerable” education apps is, in our view, a mistake. Having a unique, proprietary data moat is the competitive advantage in the AI age, and Duolingo’s 50M daily users give it a trove of language-learning data no generic model can match. This owl isn’t afraid of ChatGPT; it’s training it to become a smarter sidekick.

“Energy” Feature Backlash Fades: 

Heading into earnings, some vocal users complained about Duolingo’s new “Hearts/Energy” system (which limits free lesson attempts unless you practice or wait). Bears worried this gamification tweak would annoy users and hurt engagement. In reality, management reported that the Energy feature worked as intended, it increased both bookings (monetization) and DAUs. The CEO flatly stated: 

“Energy did exactly what we wanted, it increased bookings and also increased DAU, so that was good”. 

In other words, Duolingo’s experimental features are rigorously A/B tested, and this one is driving more usage and revenue. The negative narrative here simply didn’t pan out, yet some investors were slow to accept it.

High Expectations = Hard Reactions: 

Let’s be fair, Duolingo’s stock had been priced for perfection, trading at a premium valuation (more on that below). When any hint of slower growth or new investment (like focusing on learning quality) emerged, momentum traders bailed. The company also announced it will “prioritize teaching quality” slightly more, investing in better learning outcomes, which a few skeptics misread as a warning of higher costs or slower near-term user growth. However, management explicitly guided that this refocus will not meaningfully impact financials for the year (still expecting ~29% adj. EBITDA margins). In our view, this is smart long-term stewardship (improving the product = stronger moat), but jittery traders always worry when they hear “long-term” priorities. Throw in general market volatility and profit-taking, and you get an exaggerated sell-off.

In short, Wall Street had a minor tantrum, extrapolating a “sky is falling” scenario out of a few nuanced points. But Duolingo’s fundamentals don’t care about short-term hysteria, and neither do we. In fact, we love when the market’s emotional overreactions give us a chance to accumulate a wonderful company at a discount.

What We Like: A Wonderful Business with a Beautiful Moat 

Yes, we care about numbers. Yes, we care about free cash flow, margins, and returns on invested capital. But if we’re going to compound capital over decades, we’d much rather do it alongside a company whose mission we actually admire. And few missions are as clear, fair, and beautifully ambitious as this one:

Proven Growth Playbook (Non-Linear but Powerful): 

Duolingo’s growth has never been linear, and that’s by design. Management expertly pulls different levers (user growth, monetization, and learning outcomes) to optimize for long-term value. There have been quarters of slower user growth (often self-imposed, as they fine-tune the app or shift focus), followed by re-accelerations when they identify a new key metric to drive engagement. 

A notable example: in 2021, DAUs plateaued for a few months as the team concentrated on teaching quality; then they discovered a particular feature that turbocharged engagement, causing DAU growth to skyrocket later in the year. The lesson? This team knows how to iterate and unlock growth in spurts. It’s a “two steps forward, one step back, then five steps forward” kind of dance, and over time the trend is undeniably upward. One year ago, investors were cheering ~40% growth; this year they’re fretting over 36%, perspective matters. We trust management’s data-driven approach to continue delivering outstanding results in cycles, rather than expecting a straight-line trajectory every quarter.

Relentless A/B Testing = Continuous Improvement: 

Duolingo’s culture is often described as an “A/B testing machine”, and for good reason. The company runs thousands of experiments to constantly improve user engagement and learning efficacy. Whenever they fixate on a metric, they move it, beautifully. Case in point: during the earnings call, Luis von Ahn noted they doubled the average number of words spoken per user in AI-driven speaking exercises this year, simply by focusing the team on that metric. This ability to identify the key drivers and execute improvements is gold. It means Duolingo can adapt to user behavior quickly and optimize every facet of the learning experience (which competitors struggle to replicate). It’s like having a dial they can turn to boost a desired outcome, whether it’s DAUs, subscription conversions, or lesson completion rates, and they’ve shown they can turn those dials effectively. This gives us confidence that if growth or engagement ever wavers, Duolingo can and will tweak the formula to get back on track. Few companies have this level of product agility.

Data Moat Meets AI: 

Unbeatable Combination. We believe Duolingo possesses a formidable competitive moat in the form of its proprietary data and learning algorithms. With over **50 million daily users solving ~1 billion exercises per day, Duolingo has amassed an unparalleled dataset on how humans learn languages. This data, responses, mistakes, practice patterns, what keeps learners hooked, is rocket fuel for AI models specialized in education. In the 21st century, proprietary data is the moat, and Duolingo’s dataset is both massive and unique. While general AI like ChatGPT is trained on internet text, it doesn’t have the granular insights into effective language pedagogy that Duolingo’s data provides. This means Duolingo can train AI tutors that are better teachers specifically for languages, using its treasure trove of user interactions. We’re already seeing the early fruits: the app’s new AI-powered tutor, “Duolingo Max” (featuring the chatbot “Lily”), can converse with users and give personalized feedback. Management said that such AI features allow them to do “far more in a lot less time, at much lower cost”, accelerating the improvement of the learning experience. Crucially, these features are driving higher engagement (e.g. video calls with the AI tutor led to users speaking double the number of words). Duolingo’s moat is widening because each new AI-driven feature attracts more users or keeps them on the app longer, generating even more data in a virtuous cycle. It’s a classic network effect enhanced by AI: more users → more data → better AI features → even more users. In our view, generic AI tools can’t easily catch up to this, because they lack Duolingo’s rich, specialized data and gamified platform. The market hasn’t fully appreciated this moat-defining asset.

Financial Firepower & Improving Economics: 

Duolingo might have started as a free learning app, but it has quietly become a cash machine. The business enjoys ~72% gross margins (software economics with a free-to-play model) and has seen its free cash flow grow exponentially as it scales. 

A few years ago, Duolingo was barely breakeven; now it’s posting double-digit net margins and industry-leading free cash flow conversion. In fact, Duolingo’s free cash flow margin has trended sharply upward, by early 2024 it was flirting with ~50% in a quarter, up from almost 0% in mid-2021. This showcases the beautiful operating leverage in the model: once the company covers its relatively modest costs (mostly R&D and marketing), each additional dollar of bookings drops largely to the bottom line. Revenue grew 41% YoY last quarter, but operating expenses grew much slower, yielding expanding profits. With <$10B market cap and a strong cash-rich balance sheet (current ratio ~2.8, minimal debt), Duolingo has the flexibility to continue investing in growth initiatives (new courses, AI features, new subjects like Duolingo Math or Chess) without jeopardizing profitability. They’ve achieved something rare: high growth and improving margins, the hallmark of a high-quality software business. As long-term investors, we adore this profile.

Long Runway in a Billion-Dollar Market: 

Duolingo’s mission is “to develop the best education in the world and make it universally available”. That’s not just corporate fluff, they are tapping into a massive global demand for low-cost, effective education. The language learning market alone is huge (hundreds of millions of people learning languages, from students to professionals), and Duolingo is the undisputed leader on mobile. Yet with ~50 million DAUs, Duolingo has only scratched the surface of potential learners worldwide. Management believes they can eventually become the “default operating system for education” in various subjects, an opportunity that spans languages, literacy (Duolingo ABC), math, and beyond. This could be a multi-billion-dollar opportunity in the long run. It sounds ambitious, but Duolingo’s track record, dominating language learning, expanding into tests (Duolingo English Test accepted by thousands of universities), and early success in new apps, gives credibility to their vision. Importantly, they have no true direct competitor at scale in their core business; the owl stands alone at the top of the App Store charts. This “winner-takes-most” dynamic in a huge market justifies a premium valuation in our view (more on that next). We take comfort that management remains focused on “putting learners first”, by prioritizing outcomes and user satisfaction, they are planting seeds for organic, durable growth. We’d much rather see that than short-term gimmicks to juice a quarterly number. The market may not reward that patience immediately, but over time a truly effective educational platform will earn immense loyalty (and revenue). Every sign this quarter indicates Duolingo is making the product even better, and that’s how long-term value is created.

In sum, we see a company that is wonderfully managed, highly innovative and sitting on a widening moat. Duolingo’s fundamentals, user growth, engagement, monetization, cash flow, are all trending in the right direction. The recent drop in share price says more about investor psychology than about Duolingo’s prospects. 

Risk Factors and Potential Headwinds 🛑

Even great businesses come with risk. For Duolingo, here’s what we’re watching:

AI Competition: While Duolingo’s data moat is real, large models from Big Tech could eventually attempt to replicate its features. So far, no competitor matches its engagement loop or proprietary feedback engine, but this space evolves fast.

Growth Volatility: User growth won’t always be linear. Strategic shifts (like the current focus on teaching quality) or a natural slowdown off a larger base could lead to lumpy quarters that jittery investors overreact to.

Competitive Imitation: Language learning isn’t a winner-take-all market. While Duolingo is dominant, rivals like Babbel, Rosetta Stone, or even future tech platforms could chip away at engagement. Brand and network effects help, but need ongoing reinforcement.

Monetization Balance: Too much monetization risks alienating free users; too little risks revenue pressure. Features like “Hearts” have been net positive so far, but they walk a fine line.

Macro + FX Sensitivity: As a global consumer product, Duolingo isn’t immune to economic downturns or currency fluctuations that could affect subscriptions or ad spend.

Valuation + Volatility: Even after the pullback, DUOL trades at a premium. That makes it vulnerable to rate-driven compression and sentiment shifts. Expect volatility.

Overall, these risks are real but in our analysis, they’re manageable. Duolingo’s strong balance sheet, passionate user base, and adaptive culture give it tools to mitigate these challenges. We actually take comfort in how management addresses risks, for instance, proactively using AI internally rather than ignoring it. Still, we will remain vigilant on all the above factors.

Valuation Analysis: Premium, But Deserved 📈

After the post-earnings plunge, Duolingo’s valuation has become more attractive, though it’s still not a “cheap” stock by conventional metrics. Here’s the high-level picture:

Current Price & Market Cap: 

Following the sell-off, DUOL now trades below $200 per share (down from ~$260 before earnings). That gives a market cap near $10 billion. For a company on track for ~$1.03 billion in revenue this year, the EV/Sales is about 9x forward. In other words, ~9x NTM revenue, a far cry from the 20x+ sales multiples it sported at the height of AI hype earlier this year. The stock’s rich valuation has come down to earth significantly, even though the business has only strengthened.

Earnings Multiples: 

With Duolingo now profitable on a GAAP basis, we can consider P/E ratios, though one must adjust for the recent one-time boost to earnings. The trailing P/E is ~76x.

Looking ahead, the forward P/E is around 35x, which is high in absolute terms, but remember Duolingo is growing revenue ~40% with expanding margins. If Duolingo continues to compound earnings at 30-40% annually, that P/E will rapidly shrink. We would also note that traditional P/E might undervalue Duolingo’s earnings power because the company heavily expenses R&D and growth investments that lay the groundwork for future growth. The adjusted EBITDA margin ~29% suggests substantial underlying earnings power relative to sales.

Free Cash Flow & Cash Yield: 

We place a lot of weight on free cash flow (FCF), since Duolingo’s subscription model brings in cash upfront (creating deferred revenue) and the business has low capital needs. On a trailing basis, Duolingo’s P/FCF is ~26x, equating to a ~3.8% FCF yield. If Duolingo hits its targets, FCF should increase significantly next year, compressing that multiple further. The company’s ability to turn revenue into cash is among the best in consumer apps (free cash flow margin was ~25-30% over the past year). In a 5% risk-free rate world, a 3.8% FCF yield for a 40% grower shows the market is pricing in a lot of growth, but we believe Duolingo can deliver that growth.

Comparables: 

Frankly, Duolingo doesn’t have any true direct comparables (a testament to its quasi-monopoly in its niche). But if we benchmark against premium software/Internet peers: many mid-cap SaaS companies with ~30% growth and ~20% margins trade around 8-12x forward sales, in the same ballpark as Duolingo’s ~9x. Duolingo’s gross margins (72%) and growth profile rival top-tier SaaS firms, which justifies a healthy multiple. One might argue Duolingo’s market (education) could be lower lifetime value than enterprise software, but countering that is Duolingo’s wider moat and consumer reach (less risk of abrupt customer churn as it’s a very diversified user base). We think the current valuation is fair for a high-quality leader and bakes in some of the near-term growth moderation concerns.

Analyst Targets & Sentiment: 

Prior to this drop, the Wall Street consensus target price was around $425 per share (which implied they saw huge upside). We expect some of those targets will be trimmed given the market’s reaction, but even so, the stock is well below consensus fair value estimates. In short, sentiment got washed out in this sell-off, which often creates opportunity for contrarians.

Our take on valuation: 

Duolingo is richly valued relative to the market average, but justifiably so given its growth, profitability, and competitive position. At ~9x forward sales and ~35x forward earnings, the stock certainly reflects high expectations, yet those expectations are backed by Duolingo’s execution history. We actually feel more comfortable paying this kind of multiple for Duolingo than we would for many other growth stocks, because Duolingo has a self-funded growth model (no need for external capital), a clear path to expand margins, and a huge TAM. If the company continues to compound revenue ~30-40% and expands earnings faster (due to operating leverage), an entry around these levels could look like a bargain in a couple of years. Conversely, if growth unexpectedly sputters, there is valuation downside, but given everything we’ve analyzed, we find that scenario unlikely in the near term. In fact, this is the most reasonably priced Duolingo has been since its IPO.

Long-term investors in great businesses often have to pay a premium, but the combination of this pullback and Duolingo’s strengthening fundamentals is a gift in our view. We’d summarize the valuation like this: “Pricey, but worth it”, especially after a 20% off coupon courtesy of Mr. Market’s mood swing.
