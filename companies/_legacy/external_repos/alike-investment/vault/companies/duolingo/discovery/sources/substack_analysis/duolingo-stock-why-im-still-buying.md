Duolingo is one of the highest-quality businesses I have ever studied, and it is surrounded by a remarkable number of misconceptions.

Despite all of this, the stock is still down more than seventy percent from its all time highs. I initially bought shares at around two hundred and forty dollars, and I have continued to add to my position as the price has fallen.

What is striking about the market right now is the sudden belief that Duolingo’s fundamentals have entered some kind of catastrophic decline. There is no evidence to support that view. None. What we are seeing instead is a market in a panic state, similar to what happened with Netflix in Q2 of 2022 or Google last year. In each case, sentiment collapsed long before the fundamentals did.

This pattern repeats over and over in markets. Fear takes hold, narratives run ahead of facts, and prices move violently despite little change in the underlying business. That is exactly what appears to be happening here.

Even if you do not own Duolingo, this is worth watching simply to understand how I think about businesses in moments like this. Many of you have not seen my past successes play out in real time, including Palantir and AMD over the last few years, with Palantir being the largest win at over a twenty four times return.

I am sharing this publicly knowing I could be wrong. If I am, I will be the first to admit it. But based on the evidence in front of me, I believe this assessment will prove to be correct.

Netflix’s roughly seventy percent drawdown in 2022 was driven by events around its Q1 and Q2 2022 results. In April 2022, the company reported its first net subscriber loss in over a decade, around two hundred thousand paid users, and guided to a further decline in Q2. That guidance shocked a market that had priced Netflix for uninterrupted growth.

The sell off intensified through the second quarter as investors reassessed the post pandemic streaming landscape, increased competition, and slower revenue growth. Importantly, this was not caused by a sudden collapse in the underlying business, but by a sharp shift in growth expectations. Sentiment broke long before the fundamentals did, and the stock price adjusted violently as a result.

At the time, the media narrative was framed as follows:

“Shares in Netflix have slumped by 35% after it revealed a sharp drop in subscribers and warned millions more are set to quit the streaming service.

It wiped more than $50bn off the firm’s market value as experts warned it faced a struggle to get back on track.

Netflix faces intense competition from streaming rivals, but was also hit after it raised prices and left Russia.

Yet some cast doubt on its plans to boost growth, which include bringing in a free ad-supported service.

It also plans to crack down on password sharing, estimating that more than 100 million non-paying households watch the service this way.

In a sign of the unease, one of America’s best known investors, William Ackman, ditched his $1.1bn investment in Netflix on Wednesday, taking a loss of more than $400m.”

This points to one of the biggest misconceptions in the market: a falling stock price does not mean the underlying business is bad.

When you buy a stock, you are not buying a ticker symbol. You are buying a real business. The task, then, is to assess whether that business is actually good. More precisely, you need to determine whether it possesses the quality traits that are probabilistically and causally linked to higher rates of free cash flow per share over time. That distinction, rather than short term price movement, is what ultimately matters.

But before we get to that, let’s just look at the data: the market currently is pricing Duolingo as a dead business.

My question is this:

Does this look like a dying business to you?

What we are looking at is a business that is growing at a remarkable rate.

That growth is even more striking when you consider that management is not primarily focused on monetization or short term user growth. Instead, they are dedicating their efforts to improving the effectiveness of the platform itself, with the explicit goal of using AI to make Duolingo the best teaching platform in the world.

Alongside that, the fundamentals are clearly moving in the right direction. Revenue continues to grow year over year, and free cash flow per share is expanding rapidly. Taken together, this points to a business compounding its economic value at a very impressive pace.

“Nobody Studies Anymore – The Education Market Is Shrinking”

There is a widespread concern in the market today that Duolingo is operating in a niche, and therefore this means that the growth ahead is limited.

This claim collapses under even the most basic scrutiny.

Global language learning is enormous. Estimates frequently cited by Duolingo management and industry analysts place the number of people actively learning English worldwide at roughly 2 billion. This includes learners improving proficiency for work, travel, education, and migration. While there is no single centralized database that tracks second-language learners globally, this figure is widely used across investor presentations and industry analysis to define the total addressable market.

As of early 2025, Duolingo reports approximately 130 million monthly active users, based on company filings and earnings disclosures. These are not dormant accounts. These are people actively learning.

English is the dominant use case. It is by far the most studied language on the platform, accounting for hundreds of millions of learners, according to both management commentary and third-party analysis. This aligns directly with the global demand for English as an economic and social lever.

Now consider the implication. If there are roughly 2 billion English learners worldwide, and Duolingo serves 130 million monthly active users, the platform has penetrated only about 5 to 7 percent of the global English learner population. That is not saturation. That is early innings.

In the Q4 2024 earnings call held on February 27, 2025, an analyst posed a direct question: 

"Just given the massive TAM of 2 billion language learners, it's still really early, clearly, of the potential user base that you guys get to have. Can you maybe talk about like where you see user growth coming from next?" 

CEO Luis von Ahn's response implicitly affirmed the 2 billion figure, responding by detailing geographic expansion strategies and noting that "there's really—we're nowhere near tapped out" across regions globally

Despite operating in markets for 13+ years (Duolingo was founded in 2011), management reports no geographic saturation. In the Q4 2024 call, von Ahn stated: "Some of our most mature markets are the ones growing the fastest, which tells us we're not nearing saturation in any market. There are two billion people learning languages globally. We just hit 130 million monthly users. There's still a long way to go." Latin America, identified as the most mature market, grew DAUs at 80% year-over-year in Q4 2024.

This is why management consistently emphasizes how small their current penetration still is. Duolingo already operates at massive scale in absolute terms, yet it reaches only a fraction of the global language-learning population. The market is not niche. The growth is not constrained. The surface may look playful, but underneath it sits a vast educational market with real impact and enormous remaining upside.

As the CEO has often emphasized, this is something many people in the developed world underestimate. For much of the world, education is not a hobby or a nice to have. It is a life changing skill.

Learning English, earning certifications, or gaining mathematical competence directly determines whether someone can access better work, earn a livable income, or participate in the global economy at all. These skills translate directly into opportunity, mobility, and economic survival. For millions of people, English is the difference between low paying local labor and access to international work. Mathematical literacy underpins everything from basic financial stability to professional careers. In this context, education is about agency, not enrichment.

This is the asymmetry Duolingo addresses. The platform is not built only for casual learners in wealthy countries. It serves people for whom increased competence directly improves income, freedom, and quality of life. In much of the world, learning efficiently and affordably is not optional. It is foundational to participating in modern life.

That is why the mission resonates so deeply with management. Duolingo is not just teaching skills, it is expanding access to opportunity at global scale, solving a problem far larger and more consequential than most critics recognize.

By 2030, there will be over one billion additional secondary and post secondary graduates worldwide, and global education and training spend is expected to exceed $10 trillion, driven by population growth in developing markets and large scale re skilling in developed economies.

By 2050, there will be roughly two billion additional learners worldwide. The critical question is whether traditional education models can realistically meet that demand, both at the speed and at the scale required.

What Are Duolingo’s Moats?

Another common claim is that Duolingo has no moat and is therefore easily replicated with AI.

This rebuttal can be summarised in a singular sentence: one of Duolingo’s Moat Is that they have an Accumulated System of Iterations, Data, Network, & Brand That AI Cannot Simply Copy

First, contrary to popular belief, Duolingo does have a formidable moat, and that moat lives inside its innovation stack.

The product is the result of thousands of tiny iterations that have compounded over time. Those iterations are not isolated features, they are interconnected solutions built in response to real user pain points encountered over years of focused execution. Reproducing this stack is not a matter of copying a UI or deploying a model. It would require retracing the entire evolutionary path of the product, including all the failed experiments and subtle refinements that shaped it.

This is the same type of moat that underpins Spotify and Square. In both cases, the advantage came from obsessive iteration within a single vertical. Small improvements, tightly integrated, quietly compounded into a product that became extremely difficult to replicate.

Second, Duolingo has a powerful proprietary data moat.

The company has accumulated over 23 billion minutes of learning activity on the platform, with millions more added every day. This data is not generic usage data. It is highly specific, longitudinal learning data tied to outcomes, mistakes, progress patterns, and engagement behaviors. That matters enormously.

When a company focuses on one vertical with this level of intensity, it gains insights that are simply unavailable to general-purpose platforms. This data is uniquely suited to improving teaching efficacy. It allows Duolingo to understand how people actually learn, where they struggle, when they quit, and what keeps them progressing. That is not easily transferable knowledge.

Third, Duolingo’s brand itself is a moat.

People underestimate how difficult it is to get someone to want to learn consistently. Duolingo has solved that problem better than almost anyone, and it has done so through brand, tone, humour, and cultural relevance. That brand association is tightly coupled to learning. It is not something that a general AI platform can easily replicate.

Companies like OpenAI or Google Gemini are associated with broad intelligence and general problem solving. That is a very different product category. Duolingo owns a specific mental slot, learning made engaging, habitual, and effective. That focus is itself a competitive advantage.

Taken together, these three advantages, the innovation stack, proprietary vertical-specific data, and brand, form a moat that is far from trivial. They are especially hard to replicate for companies that are simultaneously pursuing dozens of other verticals. Focus matters.

Beyond these, Duolingo has additional qualitative moats that are often ignored. An extreme long-term orientation, unusually high talent density, low churn, and a deeply embedded experimentation culture all reinforce one another. These are intangible advantages, but they are real, and they are among the hardest to copy.

Management’s long-term mindset is particularly underappreciated. They have stated openly that the company is only about 1 percent of the way toward its ultimate vision. That vision is to fundamentally disrupt the education industry and, over time, compete with institutions like Harvard University.

This is not a short-term ambition. It is a decades-long one. Artificial intelligence makes this goal achievable in a way that was previously impossible, but only because Duolingo already has the data, the product infrastructure, and the cultural foundation to deploy it effectively.

Ultimately, Duolingo’s ambition is simple and radical. To teach people better than anyone else in the world. That outcome is only possible if you combine obsessive focus on a single vertical with high-quality, longitudinal data and relentless iteration. Duolingo has all three, and that is why this business is so profoundly misunderstood.

Fourth, Duolingo’s network is a moat.

The moat now lives in the network. Scale allows billions of iterative data points to be collected around a single use case, which can then be used to train and fine-tune large language models with a level of specificity and effectiveness others cannot match.

Just like Palantir was massively enhanced by the advent of AI - the same is happening now with Duolingo. The business is able to generate much more content, at a faster pace, and cheaper, in comparison to alternatives.

Plus, they can uniquely tune this to their customers, through the 23B minutes of propriety data collected on their platform - specifically tuned for education and learning.

The CEO explained how last year alone - the business generated 140+ new courses. To put this into context, it took the business 12 years to generate their first 100 courses. And therefore, we can see clearly how AI is massively improving this company and their ability to generate course and products with ease. This - according to management, will eventually show up in the financials and their user growth, however they note that there is always a lag.

Why Duolingo Stands Out - The Amazon Playbook:

What truly draws me to Duolingo, and what I think the broader market still underappreciates, is the company’s exceptional organizational character. This is a business that closely resembles Amazon in its early years, especially in its obsessive commitment to experimentation. That mindset sits at the core of both the company and its learning culture.

You cannot predict specific innovations in advance, but you can predict the direction and probability of meaningful breakthroughs by studying the underlying structure of an organization. Duolingo’s relentless focus on iteration creates exactly that structure. It systematically surfaces early ideas, stress tests them, and gives the best ones room to compound and mature.

This quality is one of the key traits I look for when assessing businesses. Over the long term, these organizational properties are causally linked, in a probabilistic sense, to higher rates of future free cash flow per share.

In addition, while many investors fixate on the short term, management is relentlessly focused on the long term. During the IPO, the CEO famously drew a single dot on a whiteboard to represent the IPO within the full arc of the company’s journey. The message was simple: this was the very beginning.

The co founder has repeated this idea since, saying the company is only about one percent into its ultimate vision. They have barely scratched the surface of what they want to build. I share that conviction. I believe this business has a real chance to become the best educational platform in the world, superior to any human tutor.

What that ultimately means for valuation cannot be predicted with precision, but it almost certainly extends far beyond the hundreds of billions over time. This is a clear case of leadership deliberately ignoring short term pressure from Wall Street to aggressively monetize, and instead staying obsessively focused on the long term goal of creating the best possible learning experience for the end user.

One of the most striking aspects of this company is how consistently management says they are focused on a single thing: delivering more value to the end user, and increasing the lifetime value of that user as a direct result. By their own admission, this is the worst the platform will ever be, yet it already serves over 150 million monthly active users. From here, the product will only continue to improve as a learning experience.

There are billions of learners around the world. This is not a trivial market by any stretch. Whether Duolingo ultimately captures a large share of it is a separate question, but so far they clearly are, and they are still in the early innings. From my own experience using Duolingo over the past month, that claim rings true. Learning does require motivation, but Duolingo makes the process remarkably frictionless. It is effective, and it genuinely feels like you are making progress.

In the Western world, there is often a bias that people do not really want to learn. That assumption breaks down completely when you look at the rest of the world. In much of the developing world, learning mathematics or learning English is the difference between a stagnant life and real economic opportunity. Education is a gateway into the modern economy. This is anything but a small market, yet many investors seem to treat it as one, carrying an unjustified bias against education as a category.

The central point here is lifetime value, and how obsessively focused management is on delivering more value to the end user at a fraction of the traditional cost. That focus matters because, as Jeff Bezos has often emphasized, what is good for the customer eventually becomes good for the shareholder, but only over time.

Duolingo now has a clear line of sight to building the best educational experience in the world. They can do this in a way that is uniquely enabled by AI, allowing them to deliver dramatically more value to users. That increased value not only improves retention, but also expands the size of the market itself, opening the platform to far more learners than would otherwise be possible.

What I see here is a management team that embodies many of the qualitative traits that are probabilistically linked to higher rates of free cash flow per share over the long term. These include a willingness to be contrarian, a deep long term orientation, and an exceptional focus on talent and internal retention. Duolingo stands out in Silicon Valley for having one of the lowest employee churn rates, which is not accidental.

Layered on top of that is an obsessive commitment to delivering more value to the end user, supported by a culture of experimentation and iteration that has spread throughout the entire organization. This is not a surface level strategy, it is embedded in how the company operates day to day.

The key point investors often miss is that, over long time horizons, these traits are causally linked, in a probabilistic sense, to rising free cash flow per share. That connection, more than any short term metric, is what makes this business so compelling.

When you hear the co founders talk about being only one percent into their mission, having completed just one percent of the work, it reveals the sheer scale of the problem Duolingo is trying to solve. They are not content with being a language app used by one hundred million people. Their ambition is to reach billions of users over the coming decades, and they genuinely believe that is achievable.

This is where investors consistently underestimate the opportunity. The assumption is not that every person on the planet needs to use Duolingo. Even reaching one billion monthly active users, which is their stated ambition, would represent a tenfold increase from today. From there, even if only a fraction of those users pay meaningful annual fees to access premium features, the economic potential becomes enormous.

More broadly, investors are underestimating the structural shift that is coming in education. Duolingo is already embedding itself into the education system through the Duolingo English Test, which accounts for roughly a third of revenue, is publicly certifiable, and is accepted by ninety nine of the top one hundred universities in the world. That is not a side project, it is strategic positioning.

I cannot predict the exact form this product will take in the future, but it is entirely reasonable to argue that Duolingo, by training its models on learning specific data, is building what may be the most important moat of the twenty first century: proprietary data. That data allows them to personalize and tune the educational experience to a degree that is extremely difficult to replicate.

Whether Duolingo integrates deeply into universities, school systems, or remains a consumer facing application, the opportunity in education is vast. Education is clearly broken, and it is overdue for disruption. Duolingo is positioning itself at the center of that transformation.

Conclusion:

What this ultimately comes down to is leadership. Duolingo has an exceptionally talented, contrarian, and intellectually serious management team that I genuinely trust. In most cases, that is the decisive factor. The real question is whether leadership can consistently solve hard problems with increasing speed and effectiveness. In Duolingo’s case, I believe the answer is unequivocally yes.

This is a highly competent team that I expect to continue growing the user base and monetizing it over time. Asia alone highlights the opportunity. Duolingo has barely penetrated that market, yet management has repeatedly expressed excitement about the long term potential, particularly in China, and their ability to monetize that opportunity over the coming years.

At its core, this is a business with exceptional qualitative traits, but investors suffer from several cognitive blind spots. Many dismiss Duolingo as a game or a casual language app where people do not really learn anything. I think that view is fundamentally wrong. Others argue the company has no moat, a claim I believe has already been disproven. A third belief is that education is a small or declining market, and that people will simply be distracted by social media instead of learning. That assumption does not hold up to scrutiny.

I personally do not use apps like TikTok, which I view as an enormous waste of time. By the same flawed logic applied to Duolingo, one could dismiss TikTok outright by asking why anyone would spend time on it. That kind of reasoning misses the point. As an investor, the job is not to project personal preferences, but to look at the evidence.

The real work of investing is emotional discipline and the ability to go against the crowd when the data supports it. I am comfortable doing that here. Taken together, this is a remarkably well run business with a vast market ahead of it. I believe Duolingo has the potential to become a dominant force in global education over the long term.
