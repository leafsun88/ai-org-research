Opening DUOL 0.00%↑ 

Duolingo is one of the most misunderstood businesses in today’s market.

Its playful design, light tone, and viral brand have led many investors to treat it as an “educational game” rather than a serious learning platform. That shallow reading has fueled a long list of negative narratives: that it doesn’t really teach, that education is shrinking, that AI will replace it, that growth is already behind it.

Meanwhile, the stock is down more than 70% from its all time highs.

What stands out to me is not the price decline itself, but the story the market is telling to justify it. There is now a widespread perception that Duolingo’s fundamentals have collapsed. There is no evidence of that. None. What we are seeing instead is a market in a state of panic, very similar to what happened with Netflix in 2022 or with Google more recently, where sentiment deteriorates long before the business does.

This article is not about short term forecasts or market timing. It is a simple and practical explanation of the Duolingo investment thesis: what the business truly is, why the market is misreading it, and why this gap between perception and reality creates a clear opportunity for long term investors.

The market is pricing a “dead” business

Before discussing narratives, it helps to start with the most basic facts.

If the market were right, if Duolingo were truly in structural decline, that would be visible in the operating data. Usage declining. Engagement weakening. Monetization stagnating.
None of that is happening.

Quite the opposite.

Duolingo is currently at all time highs across nearly every relevant metric:

Daily active users (DAUs) at all time highs
Monthly active users (MAUs) at all time highs
Paid subscribers at all time highs
Revenue growing year over year
Free cash flow per share expanding

This is not what a business in collapse looks like. It is the profile of a company that continues to grow, even as the market debates whether it is “already over.”

This leads to one of the most common investing mistakes: confusing price movement with business quality. When you buy a stock, you are not buying a ticker. You are buying a real company, with real users, a real product, and real cash generation. The investor’s job is not to react to volatility, but to assess whether the business is or is not creating more economic value over time.

In Duolingo’s case, the answer is clear.

What changed was not the fundamentals. What changed was the narrative. The market reset expectations, shifted into a defensive mindset, and began treating anything associated with consumer, technology, or growth as structurally impaired. That creates distortions. And when distortions become large enough, opportunities emerge.

Before talking about artificial intelligence, competition, or new verticals, it is important to anchor this point: the data does not support the idea that Duolingo is a business in decline. The price can fall. The business continues to move forward.

It is from this disconnect that the entire thesis begins.

What is Duolingo really?

To understand the thesis, it is necessary to correct a foundational error: what Duolingo actually is.

At first glance, many people see a game. Animations, humor, short lessons. From that comes the wrong conclusion that because it is fun, it cannot be serious. Simplicity gets confused with superficiality.

Duolingo is not an information product. It is a behavioral engineering system.

Education is not an access to content problem. Information has always existed. The real challenge is getting people to learn consistently over time. That is where most systems fail.

Everything in Duolingo is designed to solve this: reducing friction, encouraging daily return, and turning learning into a habit. Streaks, rewards, pacing, feedback. None of it is accidental. The goal is not to impress with complexity, but to ensure the user shows up every day.

When that happens, learning stops being episodic and becomes cumulative.

This distinction is critical. Duolingo does not compete with books, courses, or chatbots in the information market. It competes in the behavior change market. Without understanding this, any analysis of AI, competition, or monetization becomes misaligned.

The first major illusion: “Duolingo doesn’t teach”

This is the most common argument against Duolingo and also one of the most fragile.

Because the product is light, fun, and accessible, many assume it cannot be effective. Enjoyment gets confused with lack of rigor. Simplicity with superficiality. That cultural perception spread widely, but it is not supported by the data.

There are multiple academic studies and independent evaluations showing that Duolingo users achieve real proficiency gains, with results comparable to students after several semesters of traditional university education. More importantly, those results have improved over time.

Repeated testing across different years shows that learners assessed in 2023 outperformed those from 2020, and learners in 2024 outperformed those from 2023. Starting in 2023, users who reach certain scores consistently map to the A2 level of the CEFR, exactly what is expected at that stage of learning.

There is also a recurring logical error in this critique. Duolingo never promised instant fluency. Learning requires time and consistency in any system. Duolingo’s value lies in building a process that works when used regularly. Judging the platform’s effectiveness based on users who try it for a few days and quit says nothing about the system. It only says something about the user.

Finally, many criticisms still repeated today are, in practice, aimed at older versions of the product. That phase is over. With the deep integration of artificial intelligence, Duolingo now iterates faster, personalizes better, and continuously optimizes how it teaches. Evaluating the product’s future based on its least advanced version from the past is a category error.

The conclusion is simple: the idea that “Duolingo doesn’t teach” is not an analysis. It is an outdated perception. The data shows the platform works and is working better over time.

Education is not shrinking. The market is far larger than it appears

One of the most common mistakes in how Duolingo is viewed is the assumption that education, and language learning in particular, is a small or declining market. That idea almost always comes from a narrow, developed world perspective.

For a large part of the planet, education is not a hobby. It is a direct driver of economic mobility. Learning English or acquiring basic skills determines whether someone can access better jobs, work with international companies, or simply participate in the global economy. In many countries, learning is not optional. It is economic survival.

The most widely cited estimates point to roughly 2 billion people learning languages globally, with English as the primary driver of that demand. Duolingo today has around 130 million monthly active users, implying only 5 to 7% penetration of that market. This is not saturation. It is early cycle.

What matters most is that there are no signs of exhaustion, not even in the most mature markets. Management itself has highlighted that regions where Duolingo has operated the longest continue to grow at high rates, indicating that structural demand is far from being met.

Globally, education is expanding, driven by population growth, digitization, and workforce reskilling. Projections point to hundreds of millions of new learners over the coming decades and to a continued increase in spending on education and training.

The question is not whether demand exists. The question is whether traditional models can serve that demand with scale, cost, and efficiency. This is exactly where Duolingo positions itself. The market is not small. It is enormous and still largely underpenetrated.

AI does not threaten Duolingo. It accelerates it.

This is the market’s dominant fear today: the idea that artificial intelligence will make Duolingo irrelevant. That some generic chatbot will “teach languages better,” and that dedicated learning apps will no longer matter.

This argument starts from a false premise.

Most people assume that learning is primarily an access to information problem. If that were true, education would have been solved decades ago. Information was never the bottleneck. The bottleneck has always been the same: human behavior.

Learning requires showing up repeatedly, dealing with frustration, making mistakes, maintaining motivation, and persisting over time. AI is excellent at answering questions. On its own, it is not a behavior change system. Duolingo is.

That is where the distinction becomes clear. Duolingo does not compete with language models. It uses language models inside a system that already works. The company is not trying to build intelligence from scratch. It is integrating intelligence into a structure optimized for consistent learning.

This creates a powerful asymmetry.

AI dramatically reduces cost and execution time. It enables faster content creation, more frequent experimentation, personalized learning paths, and real time difficulty adjustment. All of this reinforces what Duolingo already does well: rapid iteration on user behavior.

The results are already visible. In a single year, the company launched more than a hundred new courses, something that previously took over a decade to achieve. Expansion velocity increased non linearly. At the same time, margins improved, showing that AI is not just a product experiment but a real economic lever.

The market’s mistake is imagining a binary outcome: either AI replaces Duolingo, or Duolingo ignores AI. Neither is realistic. The most plausible scenario is exactly what is happening today: Duolingo absorbs the best available technology, refines it with proprietary learning data, and turns it into a superior product.

For a generic AI model to compete directly with Duolingo, intelligence alone would not be enough. It would need to recreate years of experimentation around when users quit, what brings them back, how to set the right pace, when to reward, when to challenge. It would need to solve thousands of behavioral micro problems that do not show up in prompts or technical benchmarks.

AI makes this even clearer. Intelligence in isolation is abundant. Systems that convert intelligence into consistent action are rare.

So the right question is not “will AI replace Duolingo?” The right question is: who is best positioned to turn AI into real learning, at global scale? All evidence points to Duolingo itself.

Where is Duolingo’s real moat?

The most common mistake critics make is assuming Duolingo is easy to copy. It isn’t.

Duolingo’s moat is not a feature and it is not an AI model. It is a system accumulated over more than a decade.

First, the iteration moat.
Duolingo has been shaped by thousands of small experiments on human behavior: when users quit, what brings them back, how to adjust pacing, difficulty, and rewards. Each change is minor on its own, but together they form a system that is extremely hard to replicate. This is the same type of advantage seen in platforms like Spotify or Amazon.

Second, the proprietary data moat.
The company has accumulated over 23 billion minutes of learning data, capturing how people learn, make mistakes, progress, and drop out. This data is directly actionable for improving learning effectiveness and retention, and it simply does not exist in general purpose platforms.

Third, the brand and habit moat.
Getting someone to learn every day is incredibly difficult. Duolingo has linked learning with consistency, progress, and reward. That mental real estate is hard to earn and even harder to displace.

Finally, the scale and culture moat.
More users generate more data, which improves the product, which attracts more users. All of this is reinforced by a team with a long term mindset and a deep obsession with product quality.

What looks simple to the user is actually the output of a deeply sophisticated system underneath. That is where Duolingo’s real moat lives.

New verticals expand TAM. They are not a distraction.

The idea that Duolingo has “already peaked” ignores two simple facts.

First, the company does not need new verticals to grow. The language learning market remains huge and largely underpenetrated. With roughly 130 million monthly active users out of a potential 2 billion learners, there is still substantial room to grow both users and monetization within the core business alone.

Second, new verticals are not being launched to drive short term revenue, but to expand the total addressable market. Courses like math, music, or chess are included in the same subscription, with no aggressive monetization strategy attached. The goal is straightforward: bring more people into the ecosystem, reinforce the daily habit, and position Duolingo as a broader learning platform.

The chess example illustrates this well. It became the fastest growing vertical in the company’s history, surpassing one million daily active users, despite being available only on iOS and only in English. That demonstrates that Duolingo’s system works beyond languages.

New verticals are not a crutch or a sign of weakness. They are optionality. And well executed optionality is precisely where asymmetric long term returns come from.

Management and culture: the decisive factor

Over the long term, the quality of an investment’s returns tends to converge toward the quality of the team allocating capital and making hard decisions when no one is applauding.

This is where Duolingo clearly stands out.

Management has been extraordinarily consistent in one thing: prioritizing the product and the user over short term metrics. At the IPO, the CEO drew a single dot on a board to represent the company’s public listing within its full history. The message was simple and honest: this is the beginning, not the end.

That mindset has remained intact. The team openly repeats that the company is still “only 1%” into its ultimate mission. They are not optimizing to please the market quarter by quarter. They are optimizing to build the best learning system in the world over decades.

This shows up in several ways:

Constant reinvestment in the product, even when it pressures short term results
Obsessive focus on effectiveness, not aggressive marketing
A culture of experimentation, with low talent turnover
Rare strategic clarity about what not to do

These traits are hard to quantify, but they are precisely the ones that are probabilistically associated with sustained free cash flow per share growth over time. Companies that think this way tend to make fewer structural mistakes and learn faster when they do make them.

In a sector like education, historically slow, bureaucratic, and under-innovated, this culture is a real competitive advantage.

Conclusion: a long term thesis in a distracted market

At its core, the Duolingo thesis is simple.

The market is making several mistakes at the same time:

Confusing an accessible product with a superficial one
Confusing artificial intelligence with system replacement
Underestimating education as a global market
Reacting to short term narratives while ignoring clear operating data

Duolingo is not a game. It is a sophisticated behavior change system. It is not being threatened by AI; it is being accelerated by it. It does not operate in a small market; it operates in one of the largest markets in the world, still largely underpenetrated.

The most important point is this: education is not an information problem, it is a behavior problem. Duolingo understands this better than almost any other company. And over more than a decade, it has built a system that turns intention into repeated action.

None of this guarantees absolute success. Investing always means working with probabilities, not certainties. But when I look at the combination of product, data, culture, leadership, and addressable market, I see a business structurally well positioned to create significantly more value over time.

This is not a quarterly thesis.
It is a decade long thesis.

And that is precisely why I believe the market, once again, is looking in the wrong direction.

If you made it this far, you already understand that this is not a short term thesis or a superficial opinion. It is a way of thinking about businesses when noise increases but fundamentals remain intact.
