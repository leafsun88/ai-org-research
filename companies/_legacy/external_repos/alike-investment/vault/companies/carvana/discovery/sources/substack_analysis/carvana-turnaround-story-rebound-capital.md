# Carvana Turnaround Story

## Introduction

Once in a while, a company defies all expectations. Carvana exemplifies this phenomenon -- after its 2017 IPO, the stock surged 31 times in four years, only to crash 99% in 2022-23. Most investors dismissed the company as finished. However, Carvana rebounded from near-bankruptcy and surged approximately 10,961% in barely two years, culminating in S&P 500 inclusion.

## Business Overview

Carvana (NYSE: CVNA) operates as the digital equivalent of Amazon in the used-car market. The platform enables customers to browse inventory online, secure financing and insurance, and either receive home delivery or pick up vehicles from distinctive glass vending machine towers.

The U.S. used-car market represents one of the country's largest retail segments, historically defined by geographic fragmentation and limited inventory transparency. Carvana disrupted this landscape through three key innovations:

1. **National inventory centralization** -- enabling customers anywhere to purchase vehicles from distant locations, unlocking long-tail selection unavailable through traditional dealers.

2. **Integrated finance and insurance** -- remarkably, these services sometimes generate higher margins than vehicle sales themselves. In 2023, Carvana earned $2,407 per car from finance and insurance versus $2,385 from vehicle sales.

3. **Proprietary logistics infrastructure** -- substantial investments in company-owned distribution networks replaced dependence on third-party auctions.

## The Rapid Rise (2020-2021)

The pandemic proved an unexpected catalyst. Lockdowns rendered traditional dealership models obsolete, while Carvana's fully online purchasing process thrived. Simultaneously, semiconductor shortages and surging personal transportation demand caused unprecedented used-vehicle appreciation -- the consumer price index for used cars and trucks jumped 40.5% between January 2021 and January 2022.

Carvana's inventory appreciated dramatically, enabling sales at inflated margins. The stock exploded tenfold within eighteen months, and the company achieved Fortune 500 status in record time.

## The 99% Drawdown (2021-2022)

The federal interest rate hikes devastated demand. Monthly payments skyrocketed -- consumers who could afford $25,000 vehicles at 4% interest could suddenly only afford $18,000 at 9%. Reduced discretionary spending accompanied rising inflation. For the first time as a public company, Carvana reported declining sales volume in Q3 2022.

Used-car prices reversed course, triggering massive write-downs on inventory purchased at peak valuations. The situation deteriorated further when management acquired ADESA U.S.'s auction business for $2.2 billion, raising total debt to $9 billion amid deteriorating macroeconomic conditions.

By year-end 2022, the stock had plummeted 99%, with markets pricing the company at bankruptcy valuation. The company carried $5.7 billion in unsecured debt with major principal payments due within two years. Negative free cash flow suggested insolvency before repayment obligations.

## The 100x Comeback (2023-Present)

The critical turning point occurred in July 2023 when management negotiated a debt restructuring deal with creditors, led by Apollo Global Management. Creditors exchanged unsecured debt for senior-secured debt backed by company assets. This arrangement extended maturity dates to 2028, eliminating immediate insolvency risk.

CEO Ernie Garcia III implemented the "Three-Step Plan":

1. Drive positive Adjusted EBITDA
2. Drive significant unit economics improvements
3. Return to growth only after achieving the first two

Management executed disciplined cost reduction -- reducing workforce by approximately 20%, curtailing aggressive car purchases, and cutting selling and administrative expenses substantially. Within six months of the stock's nadir, Carvana reported $155 million in Adjusted EBITDA, exceeding expectations.

Throughout 2024-2025, continued execution drove further improvements. The company expanded direct consumer purchasing, eliminating auction fees while improving margins. The previously criticized ADESA acquisition proved valuable, enabling resale of non-standard inventory. Finance and insurance operations maintained strong spreads despite the high-interest environment, providing margin stability.

## Investment Considerations

The article notes that Carvana would not satisfy Rebound Capital's quality screening criteria. Several risk factors merit consideration:

**Insider selling concerns** -- The CEO's father, Ernest Garcia II, sold $3.6 billion in stock between August 2020 and August 2021, then another $1.4 billion in 2024, though he purchased $400 million+ worth during the 2022 drawdown.

**Accounting allegations** -- Short-seller Hindenburg Research alleged that Carvana artificially inflates profitability through gain-on-sale loan accounting, claiming the company sells subprime loans to undisclosed partners to book immediate revenue while masking credit risk.

**Related-party transactions** -- The CEO's father owns DriveTime, a separate used-car dealer. Carvana has historically engaged in complex transactions with DriveTime involving inventory, logistics, and office space. Ernest Garcia II previously pleaded guilty to felony bank fraud related to the 1990 Lincoln Savings and Loan collapse.

**Valuation concerns** -- With a current P/E ratio of 104 and elevated hype surrounding S&P 500 inclusion, the article suggests limited near-term upside potential.

## Conclusion

While Carvana's transformation from near-bankruptcy to S&P 500 inclusion represents a remarkable turnaround, structural governance and accounting concerns persist, warranting cautious evaluation by prospective investors.
