# Carvana (CVNA) - Deep Dive & Addressing Hindenburg's Short Claims

Convequity initiates coverage on Carvana with an analysis of the company's long-term prospects while addressing allegations from Hindenburg Research's short report.

## Overview

Founded in 2012, Carvana pioneered a fully online car-buying experience in the $800 billion used car market. The company evolved from relying on centralized warehouses to developing its own logistics network and iconic vending machines. A strategic acquisition of ADESA strengthened wholesale capabilities and supply chain efficiency.

Following pandemic-era volatility, management shifted focus toward profitability and operational efficiency rather than growth-at-all-costs strategies.

## Key Business Model Elements

**Distribution:** Carvana operates without physical dealerships, delivering vehicles directly to customers or enabling pickup from vending machines. In Q3 2024, the company sold over 108,000 retail units, representing 34% year-over-year growth.

**Market Position:** The company faces competition from traditional dealerships, online marketplaces like CarGurus, and hybrid retailers such as CarMax. Recent failures by competitors Vroom and Shift underscore the difficulty of replicating CVNA's model without significant capital investment.

**Competitive Advantages:** Entry barriers include substantial infrastructure costs, operational complexity, and brand trust requirements. Carvana's ADESA acquisition expanded logistics capacity to handle three times its current vehicle volume, positioning the company for improved free cash flow margins.

## Addressing Hindenburg's Allegations

Hindenburg Research published a critical report alleging fraudulent activity. However, the analysis concludes that fraud allegations lack substance, particularly concerning related-party transactions.

**Auto Loan Concerns:** The report raises valid questions about loan portfolio risk. Approximately 40% of gross profit derives from auto lending, with 35% classified as deep subprime loans. The Cerberus Capital transaction -- involving director Dan Quayle -- raised disclosure concerns, though Cerberus is a reputable firm managing over $60bn in assets.

**Counterpoints:** On January 6, 2025, Ally Financial renewed its loan purchase agreement with Carvana for $4 billion, suggesting confidence in newer loan cohorts.

## Financial Analysis

The authors estimate a 3.0-3.6% loss rate on CVNA's subprime loan book, translating to potential annual losses of ~$136m. While significant, this remains manageable within the company's growing cash flow profile.

Regarding margin expansion concerns, Hindenburg questioned how Carvana improved gross profit per unit despite 20% declines in used car prices since 2022. The analysis attributes improvements to increasingly sourced vehicles through its ADESA wholesale network, allowing it to secure inventory at lower costs.

## Investment Perspective

With only approximately 1% market share in an $800 billion industry, substantial growth potential exists. The DCF model values Carvana at $300 per share, reflecting roughly 22% upside from then-current levels.

The authors conclude that despite past volatility, CVNA remains a leader in the evolving used car marketplace, with vertically integrated model and tech-driven operations creating competitive advantages. However, investors should remain cautious regarding auto loan exposure while recognizing multiple levers to mitigate risk.
