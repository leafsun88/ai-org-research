# Carvana: Exploring the Bear Thesis Amid Recent Headwinds

This comprehensive analysis examines Carvana's business fundamentals against bearish arguments, particularly those raised by short-seller Hindenburg Research before its closure. The author, who owns Carvana shares, systematically evaluates concerns about valuation, solvency risks, loan quality, accounting practices, and management integrity.

## Key Findings

**Operational Performance:** Carvana achieved 55% revenue growth to $5.6 billion in Q3 2025, selling 156,000 retail units with a 44% year-over-year increase. The company maintains 1.7% market share while operating at roughly 5x peer growth rates and 2.5x higher profitability margins than competitors.

**Valuation Assessment:** While trading at a 300% sales multiple premium to peers, the valuation reflects superior fundamentals. The analysis projects potential 16% annualized returns over five years under conservative assumptions, with infrastructure already built to handle 1.5 million units annually.

**Loan Portfolio Risk:** Despite holding subprime-heavy loan originations, Carvana's direct balance-sheet exposure remains limited to $486 million. Forward flow agreements with Ally Financial and undisclosed partners cover $14 billion in purchasing capacity, providing downside protection through non-recourse structures.

**Debt Management:** Net debt stands at $3.5 billion with a 1.4x net debt-to-EBITDA ratio -- within acceptable ranges. The transition from Payment-In-Kind to cash interest adds approximately $60 million annually, manageable against a $660 million free cash flow run rate.

**Related-Party Concerns:** Extended warranty arrangements with DriveTime inflate gross profit by roughly 2.5-3.0%, though this represents industry-standard practice. The analysis finds no evidence of fraudulent behavior, despite Hindenburg's suggestions of accounting manipulation.

**Management Alignment:** The Garcia family retains 36% ownership despite selling $5 billion in stock, maintaining supermajority voting control through Class B shares. A $126 million private placement in July 2023 demonstrated commitment during the company's restructuring crisis.

## Conclusion

The author concludes that Hindenburg's report hits on murky-looking aspects but fails to make a convincing case. While acknowledging the valuation premium and preference for greater transparency with DriveTime dealings, the bear thesis lacks substantive proof of material wrongdoing or unsustainable business dynamics.
