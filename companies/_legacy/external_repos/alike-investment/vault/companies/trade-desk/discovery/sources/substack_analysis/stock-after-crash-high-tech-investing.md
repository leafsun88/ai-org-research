# The Trade Desk Stock After the Crash - a New Opportunity?

Stefan Waldhauser, an enterprise software veteran with 35 years of investment experience, revisits The Trade Desk (TTD) following a significant market downturn. He details his successful early investment in the company shortly after its 2017 IPO, when few believed the independent demand-side platform could survive alongside tech giants.

## Historical Context

Waldhauser invested when TTD had $300 million in revenue and a $2 billion enterprise value, trading at approximately 6x EV/sales with organic growth exceeding 50% annually. At the time, skeptics questioned whether TTD could compete against Google, Facebook, and Amazon in online advertising.

## Company Performance

The investment thesis proved accurate. TTD grew substantially, increasing revenues tenfold from $308 million (2017) to $2.9 billion (2025). Free cash flow expanded from roughly $20 million to approximately $700 million, representing a 25% cash flow margin.

## Valuation Evolution

Enterprise value ballooned from $2 billion to nearly $70 billion by end-2024. Waldhauser exited his position in late 2021 after achieving a ten-bagger return, citing excessive valuation concerns.

## Current Opportunity Assessment

Following a 70% share price decline in 2025, Waldhauser suggests a fresh investment opportunity may emerge for value-conscious growth investors seeking exposure to programmatic advertising outside walled gardens. Key considerations:

1. Revenue has grown 10x while the stock has round-tripped
2. Free cash flow margins have expanded significantly
3. The business model has been validated over nearly a decade as a public company
4. Competitive position has strengthened despite Google and Amazon's efforts
5. Valuation has compressed from extreme multiples to more reasonable levels

## Lessons for Growth Investors

The TTD case illustrates several important principles:
- Valuation discipline matters even for high-quality companies
- Drawdowns of 70%+ can occur even in fundamentally strong businesses
- Patient investors who sold at high valuations can re-enter at much lower prices
- Revenue growth alone does not protect against stock price declines if starting valuation is excessive
