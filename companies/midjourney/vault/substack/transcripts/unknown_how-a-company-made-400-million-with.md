If ChatGPT raked in money by "writing for you," MidJourney became a key player in the LLM market by "drawing for you." This report explores what made them so successful.

Our first major shock from AI was probably when AlphaGo defeated Go master Lee Sedol. Similarly, the moment ChatGPT shocked us, the underlying emotion was the same.

> "AI has beaten us."

This fundamental human fear of 'defeat' and the adversarial relationship between humans and AI depicted in numerous sci-fi movies is what AI companies leverage to make their mark in the market.

MidJourney's founder, David Holz, like many other big tech founders, is an engineering prodigy. In high school, he designed an algorithm for hand tracking using 3D modeling and was already running a design company. After majoring in mathematics and physics, he worked at NASA and the Max Planck Institute while pursuing a Ph.D.; had he completed all this, David Holz might have been AI himself. However, being human, Holz dropped out of his Ph.D. program to start his first startup, Leap Motion, in San Francisco. The equipment developed by Leap Motion was used by over 300,000 developers during the 12 years he ran the company. This company was sold to Ultraleap for $300,000 in 2019.

In 2021, David Holz left Leap Motion and started MidJourney. He assembled a team of 10 engineers and, in just nine months, created a demo of MidJourney based on OpenAI's algorithms. By August 2022, the MidJourney community had grown to about 1 million members.

Typically, at this stage, a unicorn would receive massive investments. However, MidJourney declared from the start that they wouldn't accept any investments, and they've maintained that stance.

MidJourney has only 11 full-time employees. According to Forbes, the total number of employees is 112. With zero investment, they've generated around $400 million in revenue, sharing it among a very small team. This breaks the norm of tech companies spending astronomical sums on labor, focusing instead on operating profits and model training. _Why Is This Important?_

The revenue model of LLMs is inherently centralized. Service companies using LLMs pay OpenAI, which then pays for the APIs, semiconductor companies, and whatever's left goes to R&D (with a bit of exaggeration).

The problem is the profits from this game of musical chairs are minimal. Unlike LLM players who must rely on economies of scale, MidJourney doesn't need to. Why? They earn $400 million with a maximum of 112 employees. After covering salaries and API fees, the remaining money goes into R&D and chips, leaving a profit (or so they claim).

If a company can thrive without investment, its business model must be robust. This doesn't necessarily mean it’s wise to invest, but MidJourney's emphasis on their "11 warriors" (actually 112) and "no investment" policy significantly boost their perceived value, serving as excellent psychological tools.

In conclusion, this article applauds MidJourney's market psychology tactics rather than their technology alone. They create not just bluffs but solid technology and evidence to back it up. Ultimately, they leverage the market's rule that great investments are built on undeniable solid foundations, a principle MidJourney exploits exceptionally well.
